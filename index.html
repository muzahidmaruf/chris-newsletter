The Thing No One Tells You

There are two economies. You were only taught one.

The application. The credential. The interview. That's the visible economy, and it's maybe 30% of how outcomes actually get decided.

30%

The visible economy

The posting, the resume, the org chart, the rules written down. The part everyone can see is the part everyone is already fighting over.

70%

The invisible economy

The conversation before you were considered. The room where your name came up, or didn't. The relationships, the timing, and the unwritten rules that decide who gets the offer, the seat, the deal.

The people who learn to see the system move differently. That is what this letter is about.

A Bit About Me

I didn't want a career. I wanted a genreless one.

I've led inside almost every kind of institution there is — HBCUs, the Ivy League, mid-sized privates, large public systems. I was never trying to climb one ladder. I was building what I call a genreless career: proof that you don't have to be typecast into a single lane, that the right person can earn a seat in any room they choose. So I went looking for the hardest room to test it in.

Salesforce. I didn't want to be a customer of that world — I wanted to see inside the matrix. Getting in took 20 interviews over 6 months, for a role where roughly 1 in 100 applicants is hired. I made it. But landing it isn't the part that changed my life.

The process did. Brown did. Somewhere in there I got access to what I call the invisible economy — and watched, at full scale, what relationships, timing, and a sharp set of skills can actually do for a person. Once you see it in one world, you can't unsee it in any of them.

So I never really left higher ed. I brought everything back. Today I do the work I find iconic: helping one person land the job they were told was out of reach, building enrollment, marketing, and brand strategy for institutions, and helping companies use technology to drive real change — right down to the teams who have to implement it. Career. Higher ed. Tech. The same hidden machinery underneath all three.

That's who I am, and that's what this letter is: what I learned the hard way, from inside all three worlds, written down so you don't lose ten years figuring it out.
